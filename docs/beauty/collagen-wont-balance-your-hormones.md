## The collagen boom and what it's missing

Walk into any pharmacy or health food store in 2026 and you will find entire shelves dedicated to collagen supplements. Powders, capsules, gummies, drinks — the format barely matters because the marketing message is always the same: take collagen, get better hair. It is a compelling promise, and not an entirely dishonest one. Collagen does play a role in hair health. The problem is that for most women experiencing hair loss, it is a very small role in a much larger story.

Hair loss in women is rarely caused by a single deficiency. It is almost always the result of several overlapping factors — hormonal disruption, chronic stress, nutritional gaps, inflammation, an interrupted growth cycle — converging at once. Addressing one of those factors while ignoring the others is a bit like fixing a leak in one room while the rest of the house floods. You have done something. You have not done enough.

## What collagen actually does

Collagen is a structural protein. It forms the scaffolding of skin, connective tissue, and to a degree, the dermis layer of the scalp — which does support the hair follicle. Supplementing with collagen, particularly hydrolysed collagen which has better bioavailability, can contribute to maintaining the structural integrity of the scalp environment. Some research also suggests that certain collagen peptides may have antioxidant properties that help protect follicles from oxidative damage.

These are real benefits. They are worth having. But they are limited to the structural dimension of hair health, and they say nothing about why the follicle stopped producing hair in the first place.

## The causes collagen cannot touch

For the majority of women experiencing diffuse hair loss — the kind that shows up as a wider parting, a thinner ponytail, more hair in the shower drain — the underlying causes sit firmly outside collagen's remit.

**Hormonal imbalance** is one of the most significant drivers of female hair loss, particularly during perimenopause, menopause, and the postpartum period. When oestrogen drops, androgens become relatively more dominant, and DHT — dihydrotestosterone — begins to miniaturise hair follicles over time. Collagen has no mechanism to influence this process. It cannot modulate androgen activity, support oestrogen balance, or address the follicular miniaturisation that DHT causes.

**Chronic stress and elevated cortisol** represent another major pathway. Research published in Nature demonstrated that sustained stress exposure causes dermal papilla cells — the cells responsible for signalling hair growth — to remain in a prolonged resting state. Cortisol essentially tells your hair follicles to pause. Collagen does nothing to lower cortisol, support adrenal function, or interrupt the HPA axis dysregulation that drives stress-related shedding.

**Systemic inflammation** is increasingly understood as an underlying factor in multiple forms of hair loss. Chronic low-grade inflammation can impair blood supply to the scalp, disrupt the follicular microenvironment, and interfere with normal hair cycling. Collagen has limited anti-inflammatory activity and cannot meaningfully address this pathway.

**Nutritional deficiencies** — particularly in iron, zinc, and key amino acids — are among the most commonly missed causes of hair loss in women. Hair is approximately 95% keratin, a protein built from specific amino acids including L-Cysteine, L-Lysine, and L-Methionine. A collagen supplement provides collagen-specific amino acids, not the full profile that keratin synthesis requires.

## What a complete approach looks like

Addressing hair loss properly means covering all of the above simultaneously — not sequentially, not partially. This is why the formulation of any hair supplement matters enormously, and why the number of capsules required to deliver a clinically relevant dose of multiple ingredients is itself a meaningful signal of quality.

A supplement that fits everything into one or two capsules is almost certainly achieving that through dose reduction. Hair follicles do not respond to token amounts of ingredients. They respond to sustained, adequate levels of the compounds that support their function.

A genuinely comprehensive hair supplement should address stress and cortisol through adaptogenic ingredients — KSM-66® Ashwagandha has the most robust clinical evidence in this category, with multiple randomised controlled trials showing significant cortisol reduction at a 300–600mg daily dose. It should address DHT through ingredients with established anti-androgenic activity such as Reishi Mushroom, which contains ganoderic acids shown to inhibit 5-alpha reductase. It should provide the specific amino acids required for keratin synthesis. It should include anti-inflammatory compounds — Turmeric with Black Pepper (curcumin plus piperine for bioavailability) is one of the most well-evidenced options available. And it should support the anagen phase of the hair growth cycle through ingredients such as MSM, which has been studied for its role in extending active hair growth.

## The question worth asking

Before adding any supplement to your routine, it is worth asking not just whether an ingredient does something for hair — almost anything can claim that — but whether it addresses the specific reason your hair is falling out. For most women, that reason is hormonal, stress-related, or inflammatory in origin. Collagen is not designed to address any of those things.

The supplement that works for your hair is the one that matches the cause of your hair loss. For the majority of women, that requires considerably more than collagen can offer.
